## How does a moving-coil microphone work?

1. sound (waves) cause the diaphragm to vibrate (e.g. Sound is a vibration in air pressure.)
2. the diaphragm causes the coil / wire to vibrate (e.g. The coil is attached to the diaphragm, so it moves too.)
3. a potential difference / alternating current is induced (e.g. Moving the coil in the magnetic field induces the signal., As the coil vibrates back and forth, the current is alternating.)

## Why does moving a conductor in a magnetic field induce a current?

1. the wire / coil cuts magnetic field lines (e.g. This means the conductor moves across the magnetic field.)
2. a potential difference is induced
3. if part of a complete circuit, a current is induced (e.g. Current only flows if the circuit is closed.)

## Why does an alternator produce alternating current?

1. the coil cuts magnetic field lines
2. a potential difference is induced across the coil
3. there is a complete circuit, so a current is induced (e.g. A closed circuit is needed for current to flow.)
4. every half turn the potential difference reverses direction (e.g. After 180°, the coil is moving the opposite way through the field.)
5. so every half turn the current changes direction (e.g. So the output keeps reversing direction: AC.)

## Why does a dynamo produce direct current?

1. the coil cuts magnetic field lines
2. a potential difference is induced across the coil
3. there is a complete circuit, so a current is induced (e.g. A closed circuit is needed for current to flow.)
4. each half-revolution the two ends of the coil swap from one brush to the other (e.g. This is the split-ring commutator action., It swaps connections every half-turn.)
5. the direction of the induced current / potential difference is the same every half rotation (e.g. So the external current stays in one direction: DC.)

## Why does a generator become easier to turn when disconnected?

1. there is no induced current (e.g. When disconnected, the circuit is open so current cannot flow.)
2. so no magnetic field is produced around / by the coil
3. so nothing opposes the movement of the coil (e.g. So there is no opposing magnetic effect resisting the turning.)

## Why is the output of a transformer alternating?

1. a changing magnetic field is produced around the primary coil (e.g. Because the input to the primary is AC.)
2. this creates a magnetic field in the core that changes direction
3. this induces an alternating potential difference across the secondary coil (e.g. So the secondary output also reverses direction.)

## Why does a simple motor rotate?

1. the sides of the coil experience a force (e.g. A current-carrying wire in a magnetic field experiences a force.)
2. the forces cause moments that act in the same direction (e.g. These form a turning effect on the coil.)
3. each half-revolution the commutator reverses the current in the coil (e.g. The split-ring commutator swaps the coil connections every half-turn.)
4. this keeps the forces in the same direction (e.g. So the coil keeps rotating in the same overall direction.)

- use a weaker spring (e.g. In setups where magnetic force is balanced against a spring.)
- bring the electromagnet closer (e.g. A smaller gap gives a stronger magnetic interaction.)

## How does a moving-coil loudspeaker work?

1. a current in the coil produces a magnetic field around the coil (e.g. A current-carrying coil acts like an electromagnet.)
2. the field interacts with the permanent magnet field
3. producing a resultant force (e.g. This is the motor effect.)
4. causing the coil or cone to move (e.g. The cone is attached, so it vibrates and pushes air.)
5. when the direction of the current reverses, the direction of the force reverses (e.g. This makes the cone move in and out, producing sound waves.)

## What does each part of Fleming's left-hand rule represent?

- first finger shows the magnetic field (e.g. Field is from north to south.)
- second finger shows the current (e.g. Conventional current, from positive to negative.)
- thumb shows the force (e.g. Direction the wire or coil moves.)

## How can you identify a permanent magnet using repulsion?

1. bring the object close to the poles / ends of the permanent magnet (e.g. Test each end against a known magnet pole.)
2. repulsion shows the object is a permanent magnet (e.g. Repulsion is the key test, because magnetic materials can be attracted without being magnets.)
