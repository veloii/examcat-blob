# RPA 8 — Waves

## Spec wording

Make observations to identify the suitability of apparatus to measure the frequency, wavelength and speed of waves in a ripple tank and waves in a solid, and take appropriate measurements.

### Indicative content

#### Frequency

- Use a stopclock.
- Count the number of waves passing a point in a fixed time period.
- Divide the time by the number of waves to determine the time for one wave, `T`.
- Use `f = 1 / T`.
- Alternatively, read the frequency from the oscillator.

#### Wavelength

- Use a camera to freeze the image.
- Use a metre rule to measure the distance between two wavefronts.
- Count the number of waves between the wavefronts.
- Divide the distance by the number of waves to determine wavelength.

#### Velocity/speed

- Determine a mean value of frequency.
- Determine a mean value of wavelength.
- Use `v = f λ` if two quantities have been determined.
- Alternatively, measure the time it takes one wavefront to travel the length of the screen.
- Measure the length of the screen.
- Use `speed = distance / time`.

### Extra mark scheme points from nearby questions

- Stretched-string frequency/wavelength method: adjust frequency with the signal generator, keep the number of masses the same, move the wooden bridge, observe a steady/stationary pattern, measure wavelength with a metre rule, and repeat for different frequencies/stationary patterns.
- Stretched-string tension method: add or take away masses from the string, adjust frequency and/or bridge, observe a steady/stationary pattern, measure wavelength, calculate speed from frequency and wavelength.
- To determine ripple-tank wave speed without wavelength: measure distance travelled by a wave using a metre rule, measure the time taken with a timer/stopwatch, then divide distance by time.
- Suitable apparatus for a large wavelength is a metre rule or tape measure; the ExamCat mark scheme rejected vague “ruler”/“metre stick” wording in that question.
- `wave speed = frequency × wavelength`.
- Convert centimetres to metres before using the wave-speed equation.
- If frequency increases and wave speed stays the same, wavelength decreases.

## Specific examples

- Ripple tank route: measure frequency by counting waves per time; measure wavelength from wavefront spacing, preferably across several waves; calculate speed with `v = f λ` or use `speed = distance / time` for one wavefront.
- Stretched-string route: vary tension by changing hanging masses, or vary frequency with the signal generator; adjust the bridge to get a stationary pattern; measure wavelength; calculate speed from frequency and wavelength.
- Main traps: confusing frequency with wavelength, forgetting unit conversion, and describing an apparatus adjustment without explaining how it gives a new stationary wave pattern.
