# RPA 7 — Force, mass and acceleration

## Spec wording

Investigate the effect of varying force on the acceleration of an object of constant mass and the effect of varying mass on the acceleration of an object acted on by a constant force.

### Indicative content

#### Method of varying force

- Clamp a pulley onto the edge of the desk.
- Attach a string to the trolley and place the string over the pulley.
- Attach a mass holder to the string.
- Alternatively, vary the height of the runway using wooden blocks.

#### Measurements

- Place a slotted mass on the mass holder.
- Use `W = m g` to calculate the force.
- Mark the starting and finishing points of the trolley on the runway.
- Measure the distance between the two marks with a metre rule.
- Time how long the trolley takes to travel between the two marks with a stopclock/timer.
- Use velocity/change in velocity and acceleration equations to calculate acceleration.
- Alternatively, use light gates to determine time and/or speed, with a computer calculating acceleration.
- Repeat for different numbers of slotted masses.
- A ticker-timer method was also allowed.

### Extra indicative content from the ramp-height question

- Place one wooden block under the ramp.
- Vary the height by placing a different number of wooden blocks.
- Measure the height of the ramp using a metre rule.
- Measure the distance travelled using a metre rule.
- Measure time taken using light gates and a computer/data logger, a stopclock, or a ticker timer.
- Release the trolley from the same position each time.
- Release the trolley without applying a force.
- Repeat at the same height and calculate a mean.
- Repeat for different heights.
- Calculate acceleration using `a = (v - u) / t` or `a = (v² - u²) / 2s`.

### Extra mark scheme points from nearby questions

- Use a pulley to reduce rubbing/friction from the string.
- If varying force, force can be changed by changing the weight on the mass hanger; keep the relevant mass controlled.
- If varying mass, change the mass of the trolley and keep the pulling force constant.
- Acceleration is directly proportional to resultant force for constant mass.
- Acceleration is inversely proportional to mass for constant resultant force.
- `F = m a`.
- Repeat measurements, ignore anomalies and calculate a mean to reduce the effect of random errors.
- The uncertainty in repeated acceleration values can be estimated as ± half the range.

## Specific examples

- Apparatus commonly shown in ExamCat RPA 7 contexts: trolley, runway/bench, string, pulley, mass hanger, slotted masses, stopclock/timer, light gates/data logger or ticker timer, metre rule.
- If the trolley starts rolling before release, the runway is not level; adjust the support/wooden block.
- If the string rubs on the bench, use a pulley.
- A graph of acceleration against resultant force should show direct proportionality for constant mass.
- A graph of acceleration against mass should show acceleration decreasing as mass increases for constant force.
