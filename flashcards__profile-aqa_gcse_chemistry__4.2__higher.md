## Why do simple molecular substances have low melting and boiling points?

1. small molecules
2. weak intermolecular forces (e.g. These are forces between molecules, not the covalent bonds within a molecule.)
3. little energy is needed to overcome the intermolecular forces (e.g. Melting or boiling only overcomes intermolecular forces, so not much energy is needed.)

## How does ionic bonding form?

1. electrons are transferred (e.g. This happens between a metal atom and a non-metal atom.)
2. the metal atom loses electrons (e.g. Losing electrons makes the metal a positive ion.)
3. the non-metal atom gains electrons (e.g. Gaining electrons makes the non-metal a negative ion.)
4. positive ions and negative ions are formed (e.g. The oppositely charged ions then attract each other strongly.)

2. between oppositely charged ions
3. large amounts of energy are needed to separate the ions (e.g. So ionic compounds are usually solid at room temperature.)

## How do metals conduct electricity and thermal energy?

1. delocalised electrons (e.g. These are outer-shell electrons not attached to one ion.)
2. electrons move through the structure
3. electrons transfer energy or carry charge (e.g. This explains both electrical conduction and thermal conduction in metals.)

## Why are alloys harder than pure metals?

1. atoms are different sizes (e.g. An alloy contains more than one element, so the atoms do not all line up evenly.)
2. the layers are distorted (e.g. This disrupts the regular layers in the metal structure.)
3. the layers cannot easily slide over each other (e.g. So alloys are harder than pure metals.)

## What are the limitations of simple particle models?

- does not show the forces between particles (e.g. So they cannot explain why particles attract each other.)
- particles are shown as spheres
- particles are shown as solid
- does not show movement or speed of particles (e.g. So they do not show that hotter particles move faster.)
- model is only two-dimensional (e.g. Real substances are three-dimensional.)
