# RPA 10 — Infrared radiation and surfaces

## Spec wording

Investigate how the amount of infrared radiation absorbed or radiated by a surface depends on the nature of that surface.

## Main exam question found

- Exam.cat: https://exam.cat/q/pmt-physics-2h-jun2023/01.2
- Paper: AQA GCSE Physics Paper 2H June 2023
- Question: 01.2
- Marks: 6
- Prompt: “The student wrote the following hypothesis: ‘The black-coloured flask will emit more infrared radiation than the silver-coloured flask during 10 minutes of cooling.’ Describe a method to test this hypothesis.”

## Mark scheme indicative content

### Level descriptors

- **Level 3 (5–6 marks):** method would lead to a valid outcome; key steps identified and logically sequenced.
- **Level 2 (3–4 marks):** method would not necessarily lead to a valid outcome; most steps identified but not fully logically sequenced.
- **Level 1 (1–2 marks):** method would not lead to a valid outcome; some relevant steps identified but links unclear.

### Indicative content

#### Thermometer/flask route

- Heat the water or kettle.
- Add an equal volume of hot water to each flask.
- Insert a thermometer into each flask.
- Record the initial temperature from both flasks.
- Start a stopclock.
- Record the temperatures after 10 minutes from both flasks.
- Calculate the change in temperature during the 10 minutes.
- Compare the results to test the hypothesis.

#### Infrared-detector route

- Place an infrared detector near each flask.
- Keep the distance between the infrared detector and the flask the same each time.
- Record the initial readings from the infrared detectors.
- Start a stopclock.
- Record the readings after 10 minutes from both flasks.
- Calculate the change in readings during the 10 minutes.
- Compare the results to test the hypothesis.

### Level 3 condition

- To access Level 3, the method had to allow correct consideration of a temperature decrease for both flasks or correct comparison of infrared detected from both flasks.

### Extra mark scheme points from nearby questions

- Distance between the flask/surface and the infrared heater or detector is a control variable.
- An infrared camera, thermal imaging camera, night-vision goggles or thermometer can be used to detect infrared radiation, depending on the setup.
- For absorption, the black flask absorbed the most infrared in the five-minute investigation.
- For surface conclusions: black surfaces absorb more infrared than white surfaces, and matt surfaces absorb more infrared than shiny surfaces of the same colour.
- Matt black can be described as the best absorber and shiny white as the worst absorber if comparing the table extremes.
- The greatest rate of emission occurs when the temperature difference between the hot object and surroundings is greatest.
- “Hazard” and “risk” are different: in heater investigations the infrared heater/radiation may be the hazard; burning yourself is the risk.

### Extra Trilogy mark scheme points

- Use a kettle to heat water.
- Use a measuring cylinder to measure the same volume of water into each flask.
- Use a thermometer to ensure the starting temperature is the same in each flask.
- Keep the infrared detector the same distance from each flask.
- Use the infrared detector to measure and compare the power of infrared radiation from each flask.
- Same-shape/same-size flasks control surface area.

## Specific examples

- Emission/cooling version: black and silver flasks, equal volumes of hot water, thermometers or IR detectors, stopclock.
- Absorption/heating version: black and silver/matt/shiny surfaces or flasks, infrared heater, thermometers or IR detector, fixed distance, fixed time.
- Leslie cube version: compare readings from different vertical surfaces while keeping detector distance the same.
- Main traps: not using equal starting conditions, comparing final temperature rather than temperature change, moving the detector to different distances, and making an over-strong conclusion when detector resolution cannot distinguish two readings.
