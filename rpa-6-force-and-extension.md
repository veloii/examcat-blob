# RPA 6 — Force and extension

## Spec wording

Investigate the relationship between force and extension for a spring.

## Main exam question found

- Exam.cat: https://exam.cat/q/pmt-physics-2h-jun2021/02.2
- Paper: AQA GCSE Physics Paper 2H June 2021
- Question: 02.2
- Marks: 6
- Prompt: “Describe a method the student could use to obtain the results given in Figure 4. You should include a risk assessment for one hazard in the investigation.”

## Mark scheme indicative content

### Level descriptors

- **Level 3 (5–6 marks):** method would lead to a valid outcome; key steps identified and logically sequenced.
- **Level 2 (3–4 marks):** method would not necessarily lead to a valid outcome; most steps identified but not fully logically sequenced.
- **Level 1 (1–2 marks):** method would not lead to a valid outcome; some relevant steps identified but links unclear.

### Indicative content

#### Method

- Set up a clamp stand with a clamp.
- Hang the spring from the clamp.
- Use a second clamp and boss to fix a half-metre rule or metre rule alongside the spring.
- Record the ruler reading level with the bottom of the spring.
- Hang a 1 N or known weight from the bottom of the spring.
- Record the new position of the bottom of the spring.
- Calculate or measure the extension of the spring.
- Add further weights so the force increases 1 N at a time up to 5 N.
- For each new force, record the position of the bottom of the spring and calculate or measure the extension.

#### Risk assessment

- Hazard: clamp stand, boss or masses might fall off the desk.
- Risk: injury to feet.
- Precautions: fix the apparatus to the bench, keep slotted masses over the base/foot of the stand, tighten the boss and clamp, put heavy masses on the base, or stand so you can move away.
- Alternative hazard: spring could break or come loose.
- Risk: damage to eyes.
- Precaution: wear safety goggles.
- A risk assessment/hazard was needed for full marks, although an answer without one could still reach Level 3.

### Extra mark scheme points from nearby questions

- Possible inaccuracies include not fixing the ruler in position, not clamping the ruler vertical, misjudging the bottom of the spring, or parallax error.
- Using several forces can help identify anomalous results or reduce the effect of random error; “to be able to draw a graph” alone was insufficient.
- `force = spring constant × extension`, or `F = k e`.
- Convert extension to metres when calculating spring constant in N/m.
- A graph supports Hooke’s law if the line is straight and passes through the origin.
- A spring is suitable for a balance if it deforms elastically and returns to its original length/shape, or if compression is directly proportional to force so the scale is linear.

## Specific examples

- Apparatus: clamp stand, boss and clamp, spring, slotted masses or weights, metre rule, pointer/marker if used, eye protection.
- Extension is the change in length, not the total stretched length.
- The spring should not be taken beyond the limit of proportionality if the investigation is testing Hooke’s law.
