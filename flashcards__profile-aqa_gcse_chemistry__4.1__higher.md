## How do you calculate relative atomic mass from isotopes?

1. multiply each mass number by its percentage abundance (e.g. Use each isotope’s abundance as a weighting.)
2. add the results
3. divide by 100 (e.g. Only if abundances are given in percentages; divide by 1 if decimals are used.)

## What did the alpha particle scattering experiment show about the atom?

- most of the atom is empty space (e.g. Because most alpha particles were undeflected.)
- atom has a positively charged nucleus (e.g. Shown by the few alpha particles being repelled or deflected.)
- mass is concentrated in the nucleus

## How does the nuclear model differ from the plum pudding model?

- mostly empty space (e.g. Unlike plum pudding, which had no central empty space idea.)
- positive charge is in the nucleus
- mass is concentrated in the nucleus
- electrons are separate from the nucleus (e.g. In plum pudding, electrons were embedded in the positive charge.)

## Why does halogen reactivity decrease down Group 7?

1. outer electrons / shell become further from the nucleus (e.g. Going down Group 7, atoms get larger and have more shells.)
2. the nucleus has less attraction for the incoming electron (e.g. Extra shells increase shielding, reducing attraction to an incoming electron.)
3. an electron is gained less easily

## Why does Group 1 reactivity increase down the group?

1. the outer electron / shell is further from the nucleus (e.g. Down Group 1 there are more shells and more shielding.)
2. there is less attraction between the nucleus and the outer electron
3. the outer electron is more easily lost (e.g. So reactivity increases because losing the outer electron is easier.)

## Why do simple molecular substances have low boiling points and often exist as gases?

1. small molecules
2. weak intermolecular forces (e.g. These are between molecules, not the covalent bonds within molecules.)
3. little energy is needed to overcome the intermolecular forces

## How do Group 1 metals form ionic compounds with oxygen?

1. metal atom loses one electron
2. oxygen atom gains two electrons
3. two metal atoms react with one oxygen atom (e.g. Because oxygen needs two electrons but each Group 1 atom supplies only one.)
4. ions with full outer shells are formed (e.g. This gives the formula M2O.)
