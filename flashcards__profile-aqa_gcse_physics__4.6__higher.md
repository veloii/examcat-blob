## How do oscillations in transverse and longitudinal waves differ?

- in a longitudinal wave, the oscillations / vibrations are parallel to the direction of energy transfer (e.g. Parallel means back-and-forth in the same direction the wave travels.)
- in a transverse wave, the oscillations / vibrations are perpendicular to the direction of energy transfer (e.g. Perpendicular means at right angles to the direction the wave travels.)

## How do you investigate refraction through a glass block?

1. place a glass block on a piece of paper
2. draw around the glass block
3. use the ray box to shine a ray of light through the glass block (e.g. Optical pins can be used instead of a ray box in some methods.)
4. mark the ray of light entering the glass block (e.g. Usually mark two points along the incident ray.)
5. mark the ray of light emerging from the glass block (e.g. Usually mark two points along the emergent ray.)
6. join the points to show the path of the complete ray through the block (e.g. Join the marked points with a ruler to show the ray path.)
7. draw a normal line at 90 degrees to the surface (e.g. Draw the normal where the ray meets the surface.)
8. use a protractor to measure the angle of incidence (e.g. Measure from the normal to the incident ray.)
9. use a protractor to measure the angle of refraction (e.g. Measure from the normal to the refracted ray inside the block.)
10. use a ray box to shine a ray of light at a range of different angles of incidence
11. increase the angle of incidence in 10 degree intervals (e.g. For example: 10°, 20°, 30° ...)

## How do you measure wavelength in a ripple tank?

1. place a metre rule at the side of the screen perpendicular to the wave fronts (e.g. So you measure across the wavefront spacing, not along the fronts.)
2. take a photograph of the shadow on the screen (e.g. This freezes the pattern so it can be measured accurately.)
3. measure the distance between two wavefronts (e.g. Better to measure across several wavefront gaps, not just one.)
4. count the number of complete waves (e.g. Count full wavelengths only.)
5. divide distance by the number of waves to determine wavelength (e.g. Wavelength = total distance ÷ number of waves.)

## How do you measure frequency in a ripple tank?

1. count the number of waves passing a point in a fixed time period (e.g. Pick one fixed point on the screen or tank.)
2. use a stopclock
3. frequency is number of waves divided by time taken (e.g. f = number of waves ÷ time in seconds.)

## How can you determine wave speed without measuring wavelength?

1. measure the distance travelled by a wave using a metre rule
2. measure the time taken for the wave to travel the measured distance with a timer / stopwatch (e.g. Time one wavefront over a known distance.)
3. divide the distance by the time (e.g. Use speed = distance ÷ time.)

## Why does refraction happen at a boundary?

1. one part of the wave front changes speed before other parts (e.g. This happens when the wave meets the boundary at an angle.)
2. the part still in the original medium continues at the higher speed
3. this causes a change in direction (e.g. So the wave bends at the boundary.)

## Why does an object's temperature become constant when heated by infrared radiation?

1. initially the rate of absorption of infrared is greater than the rate of energy transfer to the surroundings (e.g. So the object’s temperature rises at first.)
2. the rate of energy transfer to the surroundings increases as temperature increases (e.g. A hotter object loses energy faster to its surroundings.)
3. eventually the rate of energy transfer to the surroundings equals the rate of energy transfer in (e.g. At this point the temperature stays constant.)
